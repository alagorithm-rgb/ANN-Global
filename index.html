The Great Rebalancing: How Nations Are Legislating the AI Stack

From Brussels to Beijing, governments are drafting framework laws that will determine who controls the compute, the data, and the inference layer of global artificial intelligence.

The window to shape the regulatory architecture of artificial intelligence is narrowing rapidly. In Washington, the AI Accountability Act has cleared committee. In Brussels, delegated acts under the EU AI Act are being finalised. China's State Council published its third algorithmic governance circular of the year. And in Tunis, a working group inside the Ministry of Communication Technologies is drafting what may become the MENA region's first comprehensive AI liability regime.

What is striking is not the legislative activity itself, but its convergence: across jurisdictions with radically different political systems, the same three concerns animate policymakers — compute concentration, data provenance, and decision auditability.
